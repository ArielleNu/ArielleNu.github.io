Sheepshead Bay Through Time

Background

To me, Sheepshead Bay is home. It is the place in which I took my first steps, the place to which I have gone home to after school every day, the place in which I was quarantined during the 2020 lockdown, and the place for so much more. I have spent my entire life in Sheepshead Bay. It is the only place I know of as home. No matter what school I went to or who I became friends with, I always knew my home was Sheepshead Bay. That isn't to say, however, that my home hasn't changed over the years. I mean, I have changed, so it's only right that everything around me has, as well. Sheepshead Bay has not only changed in the groups of people that reside within it, but also in the actual structures it contains. Such alterations have resulted from both force and choice.

Sheepshead Bay is a neighborhood in southern Brooklyn, across the canal from Manhattan Beach. It is known for its strip of restaurants and stores along Emmons Avenue. Although no where near as extravagant as the Las Vegas Strip, it holds a lot of value, as it is a culmination of cuisines from a large variety of cultures. When I was young, all the birthdays took place in one of two restaurants: El Greco Diner and MOMO Hibachi Steakhouse. They were the go-tos, located only a ten minute walk from my house and nearly the same distance from my cousin's apartment. The legacy of both did not outlast the growing want for condominiums, however. MOMO Hibachi Steakhouse still remains a staple in my family's life, but El Greco Diner sold its last meal on December 19, 2014. What had been a favorite in my mom's teenage years and in my early years, was at once gone.

El Greco 2008

We continued to go to MOMO Hibachi Steakhouse, however. The unique show and close proximity to my house makes it an appropriate birthday destination, especially after my new baby cousins were brought into the world. The beating heart made of rice that is pushed up with the cook's spatula and the onion volcano intrigued all of the kids, me included. It was the same show every time, but it didn't feel like it was. Every time I came back, I was older. I was still the same person, but I had changed.

MOMO Hibachi Steakhouse 2021

Sheepshead Bay isn't just about the food. Around 5 blocks inward of the bay is a park. Only 2 blocks away from my house, Bill Brown Park was where I grew up. Every morning during the summer or on weekends, my grandmother would take my brother and I to this park. It started off with us going to the playground, building up strength on the monkey bars, holding competitions to see who could swing higher, and racing up the "big kid" slide. I broke my first bone on those swings and got hit by a bike near the gates, but I still kept coming back because the memories were unmatched. There were so many more "good" firsts than "bad" firsts. The park later became a destination for us to hold meet-ups with our friends, and play basketball or soccer. The whole group of friends, sometimes ranging in grade-level, was able to bond together through this park. Nothing seemed to matter except for the fact that we were all together.

I was often the only girl in the bunch, but that, too, didn't seem to matter. Once in a while, one of my female friends would want to come, and other times, someone's little sister might've tried to join. Regardless, I was treated like of them. We were all equal, but different.

Bill Brown Park

Coming back to that park during high school was a completely different experience. There were no more arranged get-togethers amongst friend groups. It was only spur of the moment decisions to come to the park to kill time. The park suddenly wasn't so big anymore. I got bigger and the park got smaller, at least it seemed that way. The long run from one end of the soccer field to the other wasn't so long anymore. The reconstruction of the playground that was in the designs since 2011 finally began construction in 2015. Driving home from school, I got to see the park everyday. It was becoming less and less like the park I knew, the park I remembered. Even after construction was complete in 2019, the park kept changing. Maybe it wasn't the physical construction at this point, but it was still changing. New children came to play, sharing similar experiences to those that I had as a child, but they weren't the same, and thus, the park wasn't the same. I wasn't the same. The park wasn't the same. Nothing and no one was the same. With time came change.

Aside from walks to the park, my family enjoyed walks to the beach: Manhattan Beach. I attended pre-k in the Manhattan Beach neighborhood, but even after starting elementary school, these walks continued, just less frequently. The wooden blue bridge separates the two neighborhoods: Sheepshead Bay and Manhattan Beach. Walking home from pre-k, I was fearful of the bridge. Old and under reconstruction, the bridge caused me to always be scared that I would somehow manage to fall into one of cracks, which, in reality, were way too small for me to fall through. I didn't tell anyone I was scared of it, though. I liked going over the bridge. There was a nice breeze that rushed by as I walked over the canal, connecting us to the Atlantic Ocean.

Canal 2006

The Ocean Avenue bridge has seen its fair share of trouble. In addition to complications about the materials with which it was formed, Hurricane Sandy didn't make it any easier for the bridge. Sheepshead Bay and Manhattan Beach were hit hard by this hurricane. Houses were completely flooded, cars unrecognizable, power completely shut off, and people in a state of panic. The bridge was no exception to the chaos. It was a victim of Hurricane Sandy. The bridge was destroyed and had to be repaired. It could not withstand the swift wind-speeds of the roaring hurricane. My walks to Manhattan Beach were put on pause until further notice, notice that took months to come.

After Hurricane Sandy

However, when it was finally rebuilt, I saw the opportunity to create new memories. Sure, maybe I didn't seem like the same person I was before; I was older, but that didn't mean I couldn't take advantage of my new situations in life. Walks with my grandma to Manhattan Beach turned into walks with my friends. I didn't have many friends that lived near me, but after convincing them of the uniqueness of this bridge and neighborhood as a whole, they came through and were far from dissapointed.

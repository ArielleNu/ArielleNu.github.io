My Thoughts

Why this topic?

As I mentioned previously, Sheepshead Bay is the place I have lived my entire life. Neighbors have come and go but I have always stayed. I used to dream of leaving and moving into a bigger house, a mansion, in fact. It was all just my wild imagination, however. As much as I still want to live in a large house, I have realized that even if I got the chance to do so, I wouldn't want to unless it meant staying in Sheepshead Bay. I already know all of the places in Sheepshead Bay, how to get around from place to place, and the people that reside within it. Even if one of those aspects changes, it is entirely different if all of them are taken away at once, which occurs in the case of moving to a different neighborhood.

All of this came to mind because as everyone knows, college early decision results are coming up, as are regular decision applications, which means it's getting closer and closer to the time when I have to move away. For me, that moment will most likely be my last back to Sheepshead Bay, at least in terms of staying overnight. My mom wanted to move once my brother and I got into Stuy, but it didn't end up happening with the sudden COVID pandemic. She wanted to move near Stuy so that our commute time would be less. To this day, she still wants to move, though. If my brother and I get into the same college, she will move to a neighborhood near that school.

How does this relate to our texts?

In Exit West, there was talk of the idea of "migration through time." The old woman mentioned in the text on pages 207 - 209 lived in the same house all of her life, but every time she took a look around outside of her house it was completely different. She stayed in the same place the whole time, but it was as if she had migrated. It was as if she had just arrived to a new destination. But it wasn't a new place; it was the same place. It was time that was the difference. The place she once knew had fallen victim to time, and so had she. Time is constantly moving. There is no way to stop it. All of those fictional movies where characters are capable of stopping time and perform actions without time passing paint a false reality.

In specific, over the summer, I watched a Netflix show called Manifest and the main idea of the show was that a plane disappeared for five and a half years and suddenly, the plane landed, but its passengers were the same as they were before take-off. However, everyone around them had changed. They had fallen victim to time. This reminded me of the old woman because to her, it seemed as if she hadn't changed and it was just everything around her that changed. This is because she lives in the moment, so the changes that occur to her aren't noticable in the moment. It's similar to the concept of a derivative with respect to time: dt. She is constantly changing because time is constantly changing. The change is only noticeable, however, when you look at changes that occur over longer periods of time. When the lady observed her neighborhood after a certain amount of time of not checking it out, the change seemed drastic because substantial time had passed.

The graphs above show how I think of this idea. The average velocity graph on the left has a clear delta t and delta x as time passes because the line of approximation is a secant line to the curve. However, on the right is a graph showing the instantaneous velocity, and since its measured at a point, the changes in x and t are negligible (dx and dt). The fact that the old woman isn't noting the state of the neighborhood at every instance is what causes her to notice the changing neighborhood. In either case, time is still passing and change is still occuring, as a result. This all goes to say that "We are all migrants through time."
